The history of the lake has been created from memories and stories handed down from generation to generation.

It is said that in the early days several different First Nations tribes lived in the area of the lake. Around the turn of the century, a logging company logged the area. They used the lake to float the logs down to the Montfort end where the saw mill was located. Evelyn Shields' house was the office for the logging company.

After the logging company closed, the land was sold for development. The stories that have been told are that there were various ways that the land was given away. One story was the cereal box giveaway where you would be given one lot of land in a cereal box. There were also raffles and lotteries where one would win one lot of land, too small to build on but it was hoped that it would bring people to the area to buy the remaining lots on which to build. This is where the orphan trail gets its name. There was also an orphanage behind where the Pavilion is today. To this day there are single lots around the lake that people have been paying taxes for, for over a hundred years. Many people have ended up at our lake for various reasons all with unique stories and not one quite the same.

In the past, Rue du Chemin-de-Fer was the railroad line. The first train came through around 1894 and the last train around 1962 with the tracks being pulled up in 1964. The trip from Montreal leaving Central Station would take about 4 hours. Back then this was the way that people would come up to the cottage.

There were 3 hotels, Park Inn which was located on what is now the land owned by the Gibbs’, there was another one in the middle of the village and the last one which is now Les Villas. Also, there were 3 stores, 2 with gas pumps, one at Newaygo Junction, the old butcher shop/General Store was run by the Hartman family and one where the mailboxes are in Montfort, run by the Lazanis family.

The Lake Association began in the early 1990s formally, when a group of citizens came together to start looking out for the best interests of the lake. However there have been citizens that have been doing water testing as far back as the late ‘60s.

Most of the points made here come from memories and stories, so if they are not totally accurate, please feel free to let us know.
